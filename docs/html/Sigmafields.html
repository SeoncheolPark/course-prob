Probability Triples

(Billingsley 2012)

Let

  • \(\Omega\): arbitrary space or set of points \(\omega\). \(\Omega\) consists of all the possible results or outcomes \(\omega\) of an experiment or observation.

Example 1 For observing the number of heads in \(n\) tosses of a coin the space \(\Omega\) is \(\{0,1,\ldots, n\}\); for describing the complete history of the \(n\) tosses \(\Omega\) is the space of all \(2^{n}\)-long sequences of Hs and Ts;

Example 2 For the infinite sequence of tosses \(\Omega\) can be taken as the unit interval as in the Section 1 of (Probability and Measure).

(Gut 2014)

Probability space or probability triple is (\(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P\)), where

  • \(\Omega\) is the sample space

  • \(\mathcal{F}\) is the collection of events

  • \(P\) is a Probability measure.

(Proschan 2016)

We use the triple \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)\) of probability theory, called the probability space, where \(\Omega\) is the sample space of all possible outcomes of an experiment, \(\mathcal{F}\) is the set of events (subsets of \(\Omega\)) we are allowed to consider, and \(P\) is a probability measure.

We also consider more general measure \(\mu\), not just probability measures, in which case \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mu)\) is called a measure space.

Three key facts about probability space:

(Proschan 2016)

  1. All randomness stems form the selection of \(\omega\in\Omega\); once \(\omega\) is known, so too are the values of all random variables.

  2. A single probability measure \(\mathbf{P}\) depermines the distribution of all random variables.

  3. Without loss of generality, we can take the sample space \(\Omega\) to be the unit interval \([0,1]\) and \(\mathbf{P}\) to be Lebesgue measure, which in laymans terms corresponds to drawing \(\omega\) at random from \([0,1]\).

Example 3 (A class of events which is a field but not a sigma-field) Let \(\Omega =[0, \infty)\) and \(\mathcal{F}_{1}\) be the class of all intervals of the type \([a,b)\) or \([a, \infty)\) where \(0\leq a < b < \infty\). Denote by \(\mathcal{F}_{2}\) the class of all finite sums of intervals of \(\mathcal{F}_{1}\). Then \(\mathcal{F}_{2}\) the class of all finite sums of intervals of \(\mathcal{F}_{1}\). Then \(\mathcal{F}_{1}\) is not a field, and \(\mathcal{F}_{2}\) is a field but not a \(\sigma\)-field.

Take arbitrary numbers \(a\) and \(b\), \(0<a<b<\infty\). Then \(A=[a,b)\in \mathcal{F}_{1}\). However, \(A^{c} = [0,a) \cup [b, \infty)\neq \mathcal{F}_{1}\) and thus \(\mathcal{F}_{1}\) is not a field.

It is easy to see that:

  1. The finite union of finite sums of intervals (of \(\mathcal{F}_{1}\)) is again a sum of intervals.

  2. The complement of a finite sum of intervals is also a sum of intervals.

This means that \(\mathcal{F}_{2}\) is a field.

However, \(\mathcal{F}_{2}\) is not a \(\sigma\)-field because, for example, the set \(A_{n}= [0,\frac{1}{n})\in\mathcal{F}_{1}\) for each \(n=1,2,\ldots\) and the intersection \(\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty}A_n = \{0 \}\) does not belong to \(\mathcal{F}_{1}\).

Let us look at two additional cases.

  1. Let \(\Omega = \mathbb{R}^{1}\) and \(\mathcal{F}\) be the class of all finite sums of intervals of the type \((-\infty, a]\), \((b,c]\) and \((d, \infty)\). Then \(\mathcal{F}\) is a field. But the intersection \(\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty}(b-\frac{1}{n}, c]\) is equal to \([b,c]\) which does not belong to \(\mathcal{F}\). Hence the field \(\mathcal{F}\) is not a \(\sigma\)-field.

  2. Let \(\Omega\) be any infinite set and \(\mathcal{A}\) the collection of all subsets \(A\in \Omega\) such that either \(A\) or its complement \(A^{C}\) is finite. Then it is easy to see that \(\mathcal{A}\) is a field but not a \(\sigma\)-field.

(Stoyanov 2014)

Definition 2 (Semi-field) Let \(\Omega\) be an arbitrary set. A non-empty class \(\mathcal{S}\) of subsets of \(\Omega\) is called a semi-field if

  1. \(\Omega \in \mathcal{S}\), \(\emptyset \in \mathcal{S}\),

  2. \(\mathcal{S}\) is closed under the formation of finite intersections,

  3. the complement of any set in \(\mathcal{S}\) is a finite sum of disjoint sets of \(\mathcal{S}\).

It is easy to see that any field of subsets of \(\Omega\) is also a semi-field. However, the following simple examples show that the converse is not true.

Example 4 (A class of events which is a semi-field but not a field) 1. Let \(\Omega = [-\infty, \infty)\) and \(\mathcal{S}_{1}\) contain \(\Omega\), \(\{ \infty\}\) and all intervals of the type \([a,b)\) where \(-\infty < a \leq b \leq \infty\). Then \(\emptyset \in \mathcal{S}_{1}\), \(\Omega \in \mathcal{S}_{1}\), \([a_1, b_1) \cap [a_2, b_2) = [a_1, \vee a_2, b_1 \wedge b_2) \in \mathcal{S}_1\), and \([a,b)^{C} = [-\infty, a) \cup [b,\infty)\). So \(\mathcal{S}_{1}\) is a semi-field. Obviously \(\mathcal{S}_{1}\), is not a field.

  1. Take \(\Omega = \mathbb{R}^{1}\) and denote by \(\mathcal{S}_{2}\) the class of all subsets of the for \(A\cap B\) where \(A\) is a closed and \(B\) is an open set in \(\Omega\). Then again, \(\mathcal{S}_{2}\) is a semi-field but not a field.

Proposition 1 (Intersections of sigma-fields are sigma-fields) The intersection \(\cap_{t} \mathcal{F}_t\) of any collection of \(\sigma\)-fields is a \(\sigma\)-field.

Proof. First note that \(\mathcal{F}\) is nonempty because \(\emptyset \in \mathcal{F}_{t}\) and \(\Omega\in \mathcal{F}_{t}\) for each \(t\), and therefore \(\emptyset\) and \(\Omega\) belong to \(\mathcal{F} = \cap_{t} \mathcal{F}_{t}\). Also, if \(E\in\mathcal{F}\), then \(E\) belongs to each \(\mathcal{F}_{t}\). Thus \(E^{C}\) belongs to each \(\mathcal{F}_{t}\), and therefore to \(\mathcal{F} = \cap_{t} \mathcal{F}_{t}\). Similarly, if \(E_1, E_2, \ldots\) belong to each \(\mathcal{F}_{t}\), then \(E= \cup_{i=1}^{\infty}E_{i}\) also belongs to each \(\mathcal{F}_{t}\), so \(E\) belongs to \(\mathcal{F} = \cap_{t} \mathcal{F}_{t}\). It follows that \(\mathcal{F}\) is a \(\sigma\)-field.
